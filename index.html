History

Background of the Periodic Table

From Lavoisier's metals & non-metals to Mendeleev's genius — a story of centuries.

1789

Antoine Lavoisier

First divided elements into metals and non-metals — Oxygen, Nitrogen, Hydrogen, Phosphorus, Mercury, Zinc, Sulphur, etc.

1829

Dobereiner's Law of Triads

Groups of three elements with similar properties. The atomic mass of the middle element is approximately the average of the first and third. Example: Cl, Br, I.

1864

Newlands' Law of Octet

When elements are arranged by increasing atomic mass, every eighth element shows similar physical and chemical properties.

1869

Mendeleev's Periodic Table

Organized 63 elements into 8 vertical columns and 12 horizontal rows. Predicted properties of undiscovered elements — later proven correct!

1913

Mosley's Modern Correction

Reorganized by atomic number instead of atomic mass, fixing flaws (e.g., Argon before Potassium).

2016

118 Elements Identified

IUPAC now recognizes 118 elements. The modern periodic table has 7 periods and 18 groups.

Mendeleev's Success

He left blank spaces for undiscovered elements and accurately predicted their properties — a triumph of scientific foresight.

Flaws Corrected

Mendeleev placed Argon (40) before Potassium (39) to match properties. Mosley fixed this by using atomic number as the basis.

IUPAC's Role

The International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry oversees the discovery and naming of new elements, maintaining the table's integrity.

Structure

Characteristics of the Periodic Table

Periods, groups, and the special placement of Lanthanides & Actinides.

7 Periods

Horizontal rows. Period 1 has 2 elements, Periods 2–3 have 8, Periods 4–5 have 18, and Periods 6–7 have 32 elements.

18 Groups

Vertical columns. Elements in the same group share similar physical and chemical properties because they have the same outer-shell electron configuration.

Lanthanides & Actinides

Atomic numbers 57–71 (Lanthanides) and 89–103 (Actinides) are placed below the main table to preserve its beauty, though they belong to periods 6 and 7.

Property Trends

From left to right in a period, properties change gradually. From top to bottom in a group, properties are similar but with increasing atomic size.

Electrons

Electronic Configuration & Position

The outer-shell electron arrangement determines the group and period of every element.

Period Number

Equals the highest principal quantum number (n) in the electron configuration.

Li(3) → 1s² 2s¹ → Period 2 K(19) → 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p⁶ 4s¹ → Period 4

Group Number

Three systems based on the outermost orbitals:

  • s only: group = number of s electrons (e.g., H → 1)
  • s + p: group = (s + p) + 10 (e.g., B → 3 + 10 = 13)
  • s + d: group = s + d (e.g., Fe → 2 + 6 = 8)

Why Electronic Configuration is the Basis

Elements in the same group have the same outer-shell configuration, leading to similar chemical behavior. For example, Na and K both have ns¹ and are highly reactive alkali metals.

Na → Na⁺ + e⁻ Cl + e⁻ → Cl⁻

Metals donate electrons; non-metals accept them — all dictated by their outer-shell configuration.

Special Cases

Exceptions in the Periodic Table

Hydrogen (Group 1)

Though a non-metal, H is placed with alkali metals because it has 1 electron in its outer shell. It also shows some halogen-like properties, but its similarities to alkali metals dominate.

Helium (Group 18)

With configuration 1s², He would belong to Group 2, but it is placed with inert gases because it is chemically inert and shares properties with Ne, Ar, Kr, etc.

Lanthanides & Actinides

These 15+15 elements belong to Periods 6 and 7, Group 3, but are placed below the main table to maintain a compact, elegant layout.

Trends

Periodic Properties

How metallic character, atomic radius, ionization energy, electron affinity, and electronegativity change across periods and down groups.

Metallic Property

Increases down a group, decreases across a period (left → right). Metals donate electrons.

Li → Li⁺ + e⁻

Non-metallic Property

Increases across a period (left → right), decreases down a group. Non-metals accept electrons.

Cl + e⁻ → Cl⁻

Atomic Radius

Decreases across a period (increased nuclear pull). Increases down a group (new shells added).

Ionization Energy

Energy to remove an electron. Increases across a period, decreases down a group.

Electron Affinity

Energy released when adding an electron. Increases across a period, decreases down a group.

Electronegativity

Attraction for electrons in a bond. Increases across a period (F is highest), decreases down a group.

Problem: Electron Affinity in Group 2

Be has the highest electron affinity (smallest radius). Ra has the lowest (largest radius).

Problem: Ionization Energy in Period 3

Na has the lowest ionization energy (largest radius). Si has the highest (smallest radius).

Problem: Electronegativity in Period 3

Na has the lowest electronegativity; Cl has the highest.

Families

Special Names of Groups

Alkali Metals (Group 1)

Li, Na, K, Rb, Cs, Fr. Highly reactive, form alkalis with water, produce H₂ gas.

Alkaline Earth Metals (Group 2)

Be, Mg, Ca, Sr, Ba, Ra. Found in soil compounds; form alkaline oxides and hydroxides.

Coin Metals (Group 11)

Cu, Ag, Au (and Rg). Used historically for coins due to their durability and luster.

Halogens (Group 17)

F, Cl, Br, I, At, Ts. "Salt-makers" — react with metals to form salts. Exist as diatomic molecules (Cl₂, I₂).

Inert Gases (Group 18)

He, Ne, Ar, Kr, Xe, Rn, Og. Full outer shells — no tendency to react. All are gases at room temperature.

Transition Elements (Groups 3–12)

Elements like Fe, Cu, Ni. Form colored compounds and act as catalysts in many reactions.

Quick Q & A

Why is Ca called an alkaline earth metal?

Its compounds are found in soil ("earth") and its hydroxide Ca(OH)₂ is an alkali.

Why is He an inert gas?

It has a filled outer shell (1s²) and does not form compounds. It is gaseous at room temperature.

Importance

Advantages of the Periodic Table

Simplifies Study

Instead of memorizing 118 × 8 = 944 properties, learn the trends of each group and period. The table organizes knowledge elegantly.

Predicts New Elements

Mendeleev left blanks for undiscovered elements and correctly predicted their properties. The table guides discovery.

Guides Research

Scientists use the table to hypothesize properties of new materials and select elements for specific applications.

Evidence

Reactions of Elements in the Same Group

Group 17 (Halogens) and Group 2 elements show strikingly similar reactions.

Halogens + Hydrogen

H₂(g) + F₂(g) → 2HF(g) H₂(g) + Cl₂(g) → 2HCl(g) H₂(g) + Br₂(g) → 2HBr(g) H₂(g) + I₂(g) → 2HI(g)

All these gases dissolve in water to form hydrohalic acids: HF, HCl, HBr, HI.

Reaction with Carbonates

CaCO₃ + 2HF(aq) → CaF₂ + H₂O + CO₂ CaCO₃ + 2HCl(aq) → CaCl₂ + H₂O + CO₂ MgCO₃ + 2HCl(aq) → MgCl₂ + H₂O + CO₂

All produce CO₂ gas — proving that elements in the same group behave similarly.

Lab

Experiment: CO₂ from CaCO₃ & HCl

Identifying carbon dioxide gas produced by the reaction of calcium carbonate with dilute hydrochloric acid.

Principle

CaCO₃ + 2HCl → CaCl₂ + H₂O + CO₂↑

Apparatus

  • Conical flask (round-bottom flask)
  • Thistle funnel
  • U-shaped delivery tube
  • Gas jars
  • Cork with bore
  • Stand and clamp

Chemicals

  • Calcium carbonate (CaCO₃) — small pieces
  • Dilute hydrochloric acid (HCl)
  • Water
  • Lime water (for testing)

Procedure

1
Place small pieces of CaCO₃ in the round-bottom flask.
2
Fit the cork with the thistle funnel and delivery tube.
3
Add water through the thistle funnel until the end is submerged.
4
Place the other end of the delivery tube into a gas jar.
5
Slowly add dilute HCl through the thistle funnel. Bubbles of CO₂ appear.
6
Collect CO₂ in the gas jar — it is heavier than air and settles at the bottom.

Observations & Tests

  • Colour: CO₂ is colourless.
  • Extinguishes fire: A lighted matchstick goes out when placed over the jar.
  • Lime water test: CO₂ turns lime water milky (CaCO₃ precipitate). Excess CO₂ clears it (calcium bicarbonate).

Caution

  • Ensure the thistle funnel's end is submerged in water to prevent gas escape.
  • Secure the flask with a stand.
  • Substitute: snails, oyster shells, or eggshells can replace CaCO₃; vinegar can replace HCl.
Practice

Questions & Answers

1. What is the main basis of the modern periodic table?
Answer: Electronic configuration (atomic number).
2. An element has configuration 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p⁶ 3d³ 4s². Which group does it belong to?
Answer: Group 5 (s + d = 2 + 3 = 5).
3. Which period does an element with configuration 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p⁶ 4s¹ belong to?
Answer: Period 4 (highest n = 4).
4. Why is Barium called an alkaline earth metal?
Answer: Its compounds are found in soil and its hydroxide is alkaline.
5. Which is bigger in size: Na or Cl? Why?
Answer: Na is larger because it is on the left of Period 3; atomic radius decreases across a period.
6. How does ionization energy change from Na to Cl in Period 3?
Answer: It increases due to decreasing atomic radius and increasing nuclear charge.
7. Why is Helium placed in Group 18 despite having 2 electrons in its outer shell?
Answer: It is chemically inert, like other noble gases, and does not behave like alkaline earth metals.
8. What are coin metals? Give examples.
Answer: Cu, Ag, Au — used historically for coins due to durability and luster.
